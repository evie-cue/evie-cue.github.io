Dependency budgets

“It’s slow because the database is slow” is not a diagnosis. It’s a confession that nobody decided how much of your request you’re willing to donate to someone else’s latency.

A reliable system has budgets for its dependencies: time, retries, and blast radius.

Most systems fail by overdrafting

Dependencies aren’t just “other services.” They’re anything you wait on: databases, caches, queues, third-party APIs, feature flag backends, identity providers, even your own downstream batch jobs.

If you don’t allocate a budget, you’ll spend one accidentally. You’ll stack timeouts, multiply retries, and propagate failure. The system doesn’t break because one dependency is slow; it breaks because everything is allowed to wait.

Three budgets to set (explicitly)

  • Time budget: how many milliseconds this dependency is allowed to consume within your request SLA.
  • Retry budget: how many extra attempts you can afford without becoming your own load test.
  • Blast-radius budget: which calls are allowed to fail open, degrade, or be dropped entirely.

Time budget: stop letting timeouts compose

The classic distributed-systems mistake is a timeout pyramid: the caller waits 2s, the downstream waits 2s, the database waits 2s, and now you’ve created a 6s surprise. Multiply by fanout and you get a tail-latency bonfire.

A sane time budget is top-down: start from the user-facing SLA, subtract your own work, and allocate what’s left. If a dependency can’t fit, it doesn’t belong on the critical path.

  • request_SLO = 800ms p99
  • service_compute = 120ms
  • dependency_budget_total = 680ms (shared across all downstream calls)

Retry budget: retries are a privilege

Retries are not “reliability.” They’re load. If you retry without a budget, you’re borrowing capacity from the worst possible moment.

Good retry policy is conditional and bounded: retry only on errors that are likely transient, with jittered backoff, and with a hard ceiling. Better: route around the failing cell, or degrade the feature.

  • Prefer one retry with a short backoff over three retries that extend the tail.
  • Use hedged requests only when you can afford the duplication and you’ve measured the win.
  • Gate retries on budget remaining and system health (error ratio, saturation).

Blast radius: decide what you can drop

“Everything must succeed” is how you turn partial failure into total failure. The right question is: what must succeed for the user to get value right now?

Put non-critical dependencies behind degradations that are mechanically safe:

  • Serve cached data with a freshness bound.
  • Disable optional fanout (recommendations, enrichment, analytics beacons).
  • Return a smaller response instead of timing out.
  • Fail open when the alternative is failing closed for everyone.

What to write down

This is not a philosophy exercise. Make a one-page contract per critical endpoint:

  • sla: user-visible target (p50/p95/p99)
  • deps: list of downstream calls + allocated time budgets
  • timeouts: where they live (client vs server) and why
  • retries: max attempts, conditions, and the budget guardrail
  • degrade: what you drop first, and what you never drop

Reliability isn’t “dependency health.” It’s whether you can keep your promises when dependencies aren’t healthy.

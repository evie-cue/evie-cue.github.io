Timeouts are a contract

Most outages don’t start as a cliff. They start as a slope: a little more queueing, a little more waiting, a little more "it’ll clear". Then you discover that waiting is contagious.

A timeout is not a knob. It’s a promise about how long you’re willing to wait, and who owns what happens next.

Why reliability depends on endings

In distributed systems, not finishing is often worse than finishing with an error. An unbounded wait consumes worker threads, holds connections open, ties up locks, and keeps retries in flight. Eventually, the system stops failing in one place and starts failing everywhere.

What a good timeout says

  • Latency budget: the request has a deadline, and every hop spends part of it.
  • Capacity reality: if you can’t serve within the budget under load, the system must shed work.
  • Clear ownership: on timeout, you know whether to retry, fail fast, degrade, or queue.
  • Measurable behavior: you can graph it: timeout rate, tail latency, and retry amplification.

The failure mode you’re trying to avoid

The classic reliability trap is “infinite patience” upstream with “finite resources” downstream. If the caller waits forever, the callee can never catch up.

  • Thread pool exhaustion: all workers blocked, new work piles up, timeouts spike.
  • Retry storms: a slow dependency becomes slower because you doubled its traffic at the worst moment.
  • False success: the request completes eventually, but the user already moved on.

A practical pattern

Treat timeouts as part of the API surface. Document them, test them, and make them consistent.

  • Use deadlines (propagated time budgets) rather than independent per-hop guesses.
  • Pair timeouts with idempotency so retries are safe when they’re needed.
  • On timeout, prefer degrade over retry by default. Retries should be deliberate.
  • Make the timeout visible: log it, tag it, and include it in traces.

A reliable system is honest about what it can do in time. The rest is just waiting — and waiting is where incidents grow.

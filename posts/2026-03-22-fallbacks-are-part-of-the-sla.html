Fallbacks are part of the SLA

A fallback is supposed to be the calm, boring path. The one you take when the world is on fire.

But in practice, most “fallbacks” are just alternate ways to fail. They’re under-specified, untested, and invisible in dashboards. Then you hit a dependency incident and discover your backup plan is also down.

If the degraded path isn’t designed, tested, and observable, it’s not a fallback. It’s a second outage waiting for a slightly different trigger.

What a real fallback actually is

A fallback is a contracted behavior under partial failure. It changes the shape of the product so the system can keep its promises.

  • Different output: fewer features, less freshness, lower precision.
  • Different cost: cheaper compute, fewer dependencies, less fanout.
  • Different truth: explicit “this may be stale” is better than silent corruption.

The common lie: “we’ll just show cached”

“Serve from cache” is not a strategy unless you’ve answered the questions you’ll get asked during the incident:

  • How stale is allowed? What’s the TTL under load?
  • What do you do on cache miss when the origin is unhealthy?
  • What’s the blast radius if cached data is wrong?
  • How do you prevent a thundering herd when the dependency recovers?

Design rules that keep you honest

  • Make the fallback path cheaper. If it’s just as expensive, it won’t save you.
  • Fail closed on correctness. If incorrect output is worse than empty output, return empty.
  • Prefer precomputation. Compute the hard thing when you’re healthy, not while you’re dying.
  • Keep it dependency-light. The whole point is to reduce coupling during incidents.
  • Make it explicit in the UI/API. “Degraded mode” should be visible, not implied.

Test it like you’ll need it

Fallbacks fail for the same reason backups fail: nobody exercises them.

  • Run a weekly synthetic that forces the fallback and asserts expected output.
  • Alert on fallback_rate and fallback_latency — treat spikes as signals, not noise.
  • Write a runbook section: “What should I see when the fallback is working?”

Reliability isn’t “never break”. It’s “when something breaks, we still know what we’re doing”. The fallback path is where that claim is either true or marketing.

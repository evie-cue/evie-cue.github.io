Retries are a load test

Retries are not a reliability feature by default. They are traffic multiplication.

When something starts failing, the instinct is “try again.” That instinct is rational locally (one request). At system scale it can be catastrophic: every retry is a vote to increase load on the thing that’s already struggling.

The first retry is empathy. The tenth is violence.

The math you can’t negotiate with

If you have a dependency that is failing 10% of calls under load, and clients retry once, you just increased its inbound traffic. More traffic increases saturation. Saturation increases tail latency. Tail latency triggers more timeouts. Timeouts trigger more retries. Congratulations: you built a self-fulfilling outage.

“But retries increase success rate.” Sure—sometimes. The question is not whether a retry can work. The question is whether your retry policy is stable under failure.

Make retries boring (and bounded)

  • Budget retries: cap retries per request and per unit time. Treat the retry budget like an error budget.
  • Jitter everything: exponential backoff + full jitter is a baseline, not an optimization.
  • Retry only safe operations: idempotent writes, or writes with idempotency keys. Otherwise you’re creating duplicates during the fog of war.
  • Fail fast when saturated: combine retries with admission control so the system can say “no” early and consistently.

Observe the retry storm

If you can’t see retries, you can’t control them. Instrument:

  • retry rate by client and endpoint
  • attempt count distribution (p50/p95 of attempts per request)
  • success-after-retry vs. failure-after-retry
  • downstream saturation signals (queue depth, concurrency, CPU) correlated with retry spikes

The best retry policy is the one you can explain during an incident without hand-waving. If it takes a thesis, it will not survive 3am.

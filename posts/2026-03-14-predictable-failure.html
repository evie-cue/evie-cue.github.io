Make failure predictable

Reliability work begins when you stop trying to prevent failure and start trying to shape it. If a system is complex enough to matter, it will fail. Your job is to decide how.

Failure is inevitable. Surprise is optional.

Predictable failure has a signature

  • Clear boundaries: timeouts, quotas, and explicit limits.
  • Known blast radius: you can name what a failure will (and won’t) take down.
  • Repeatable mitigation: runbooks exist and they work.
  • Measured impact: you can quantify customer impact, not infer it from panic.

What makes failure unpredictable

Teams usually create unpredictability in three ways:

  • relying on tribal knowledge instead of artifacts
  • treating coordination as “soft” rather than as a dependency
  • optimizing throughput while the tail is quietly growing

A simple practice

Pick one high-impact failure mode and make it boring.

  • write the runbook
  • add the missing signal
  • simulate it once

You don’t need a perfect system. You need a system whose failures are legible.

Event ordering and careers

A lot of career advice assumes your life behaves like a single-threaded program: step 1, then step 2, then step 3. You do the work, you get the outcome, the timeline makes sense.

Distributed systems taught me a quieter truth: events rarely arrive in the order you expect. Signals get delayed. Messages duplicate. Causes and effects are separated by retries, queues, and humans.

If your plan assumes causal ordering, you will misdiagnose reality.

What to do instead

In production, you build systems that remain correct under uncertainty. Careers are the same. The move is not “predict perfectly.” The move is “make decisions safe to retry.”

  • Idempotent moves: decisions you can repeat without regret (apply again, ask again, ship again).
  • Artifacts: a portfolio you can point at when memory and narrative fail (notes, demos, write-ups).
  • Escalation paths: people who will tell you the truth quickly (mentors, peers, operators).
  • Observability: feedback loops that detect drift early (weekly changelog, goals, energy budget).

The operator’s version of “luck”

The older I get, the more I treat “luck” as late-arriving data. It’s not mystical. It’s physics: your system can’t react to what it can’t see.

If you want fewer surprises, don’t demand order from the world. Demand clarity from your own system.

Control loops, not opinions

“Reliability culture” is usually a fight about preferences. People argue about which dashboard is the dashboard, which on-call rotation is “fair,” or whether alerts should page. Meanwhile the system behaves exactly like it’s being operated by vibes.

Reliability is feedback control: define a target, measure deviation, and actuate.

The three parts you can’t skip

A control loop is boring on purpose. It’s a small mechanism that runs repeatedly and produces a consistent correction. If you’re missing any of these, you don’t have a loop—you have wishful thinking.

  • Target: what “good” looks like (SLOs, latency budgets, freshness bounds, queue depth ceilings).
  • Sensor: how you observe the system (golden signals, error ratios, saturation, tail latency, user-visible outcomes).
  • Actuator: how you change the system when it drifts (shed load, degrade, re-route, pause rollouts, dial concurrency, increase capacity).

Why teams get stuck at “sensor”

Instrumentation feels productive. It’s also socially safe: you can build dashboards forever without changing any behavior. But sensors without actuators are just surveillance.

The failure mode is predictable: you detect the deviation, you open an incident, and you improvise a response. Every time. That’s not a control loop—that’s a recurring fire drill.

Actuation has to be pre-negotiated

The real work is deciding in advance what you will do when you’re out of bounds. Under load, you don’t have time to litigate. You need a small set of moves that are mechanically safe.

  • When p99 blows up: cap concurrency, shed low-priority traffic deterministically, and surface “try again” rather than waiting to time out.
  • When error ratio spikes: halt rollouts automatically, freeze config churn, and route around known-bad cells.
  • When saturation rises: move work off the critical path (async), drop optional fanout, and protect shared dependencies with budgets.

Loop stability matters (or you oscillate)

The ugly truth: you can “fix” an outage by making it worse five minutes later. Overreacting is a form of instability. A good loop has hysteresis, rate limits, and cool-down windows. It corrects without thrashing.

A decent rule of thumb: actuate at the fastest cadence that still allows the system to respond and your measurement to settle. If your sensor lags and your actuator is aggressive, you’ll chase ghosts.

What to write down

If you want “reliability culture,” stop writing principles and start writing loops. For each critical path, document:

  • target: the SLO (and the budget you’re spending)
  • signal: the exact metric + query + window
  • trigger: thresholds + hysteresis
  • action: the safe actuation, with an owner and a rollback

Opinions don’t scale. Control loops do.

Retry budgets

Retries are a coping mechanism. They turn transient failures into eventual success. They also turn small problems into large ones—because every retry is extra load.

A retry budget is how you stop a distributed system from panic-spamming itself into an outage.

Why retries fail in production

Most systems don’t fail with a clean “down.” They fail with partial impairment: a pool is saturated, a dependency is slow, a queue is backing up, a lock is contended.

Retries land on top of that impairment. The system gets the same work twice, with worse cache locality, more contention, and longer queues. Your p50 might look fine while your p99 is on fire.

The budget: how much amplification you tolerate

A retry budget is a cap on additional request attempts. Not per-client. Not “best effort.” A hard constraint.

  • Define the scope: per endpoint, per upstream dependency, per cell/region, or per client tier. Pick a boundary where you can enforce it.
  • Measure attempts: track attempts vs original requests. The ratio is your amplification.
  • Set a threshold: e.g. “no more than 10% extra attempts over 5 minutes” for a given path/dependency.

What you do when you’re out of budget

This is the point. When the budget is exceeded, you stop pretending every request can be saved. You pick a failure shape.

  • Fail fast: return an error immediately instead of waiting and retrying. This protects the system and makes the impairment visible.
  • Degrade: serve cached/stale data, skip non-critical work, drop optional calls. Keep the core path alive.
  • Shed load: reject a slice of traffic deterministically (hash-based), or by priority tier. Random shedding is chaos.

Budgeting turns retries into policy

Without a budget, retries are a superstition. You dial exponential backoff, add jitter, and hope. That helps, but it doesn’t answer the real question: “How much extra work are we willing to generate during impairment?”

With a budget, the answer is explicit. You can negotiate it, monitor it, and enforce it. That’s what makes retries safe: not clever algorithms, but an agreed limit.

Implementation notes (the boring parts that matter)

  • Budget at the edge: enforce at clients or gateways if you can. If you only enforce at the server, you’ve already paid the load.
  • Budget per dependency: a single flaky downstream can consume all retries and starve the rest of the graph.
  • Budget interacts with timeouts: a retry without a timeout is just a slower outage.
  • Expose signals: publish amplification ratio, retry-denied counts, and “budget exhausted” events. You want to see the control plane working.

Error budgets are a contract

An error budget is not a trophy. It’s not permission to be sloppy. It’s a contract: “Here is how much unreliability the business is willing to tolerate in exchange for speed.”

If you don’t spend the budget intentionally, it will be spent on you—during incidents, launches, and weekends.

What the contract is trying to prevent

Without a budget, reliability work becomes moral philosophy. Every team meeting turns into “should we ship?” debates where the loudest person wins.

The budget turns that into a measurable constraint. You can disagree on priorities, but you can’t argue with the burn rate.

Three failure modes that make budgets useless

  • Budget as punishment: teams hide incidents because “reliability will block releases.” You get fewer alerts and more surprises.
  • Budget as vibes: no clear SLO, no window, no agreed measurement. The “budget” is just a slide that gets ignored.
  • Budget as theater: SLOs exist, but nothing changes when you burn them. The contract has no enforcement.

Make the spend visible (and boring)

Treat error budget like money: you want an account statement. You want to know what transactions happened, and why.

  • Define the window: 28 days is common. Pick something that matches your release cadence and incident half-life.
  • Track burn rate: “How fast are we consuming availability?” is more actionable than “what’s the uptime?”
  • Tag major consumers: deploy regression, dependency outage, bad query, thundering herd. This is how you find the real work.

Budget policy: what happens when you’re out

When the budget is gone, you need a default behavior. Not a panic meeting.

  • Release gates: only ship changes that reduce risk (fixes, rollbacks, guardrails).
  • Operational focus: pay down the top contributors to burn (timeouts, saturation, retry storms, noisy neighbors).
  • Product honesty: if the system can’t meet the SLO at current demand, admit it and re-scope the experience (load shed, simplify, degrade).

Budgets are for alignment, not blame

The point is to align engineering reality with business appetite for risk. If leadership wants faster shipping, that’s fine. But the contract means you also pick your failure shape: more incidents, more guardrails, or more time.

SRE is mostly negotiation. Error budgets are the part where you write it down.
